Breathing and Grounding Exercises

Sit or lie down comfortably.
Place one hand on your chest and the other on your abdomen.
Inhale deeply through your nose, letting your abdomen rise while keeping your chest relatively still.
Exhale slowly through your mouth, letting your abdomen fall.
Repeat several times, focusing on the sensation of your breath.
Inhale quietly through your nose for a count of 4.
Hold your breath for a count of 7.
Exhale completely through your mouth for a count of 8, making a whooshing sound.
Repeat this cycle for a few rounds.
Inhale slowly through your nose for a count of 4.
Hold your breath for a count of 4.
Exhale slowly through your mouth for a count of 4.
Hold your breath again for a count of 4.
Repeat the cycle, visualizing each side of a square as you breathe.
Find a quiet place and sit or lie down comfortably.
Close your eyes and bring your attention to your breath.
Notice the sensation of each inhale and exhale without trying to change it.
If your mind wanders, gently bring your focus back to your breath.
Practice for several minutes, gradually increasing the duration as you become more comfortable.
Sit quietly and close your eyes.
Inhale slowly and count each breath in your mind, starting from 1 up to 10.
If you lose count or get distracted, simply start over from 1.
Combine deep breathing with progressive relaxation of muscle groups.
Start with your toes and work your way up, tensing each muscle group as you inhale and relaxing as you exhale.
This can help release physical tension associated with anxiety.
Name 5 things you can see around you.
Name 4 things you can touch or feel (e.g., the texture of your clothing, the ground beneath your feet).
Name 3 things you can hear right now.
Name 2 things you can smell or that bring you comfort (if possible).
Name 1 thing you like about yourself or feel grateful for.
This technique helps to shift focus away from anxiety and back to the present moment.
Sit or stand comfortably and take a few deep breaths.
Notice your surroundings without judgment.
Pay attention to the colors, shapes, and textures of objects around you.
Listen to the sounds in your environment, both near and far.
Feel the sensations of your body touching the ground or the chair.
This practice helps to anchor you in the present moment.
Carry a small object with you (e.g., a smooth stone, a keychain, a piece of fabric).
When you feel anxious, hold the object in your hand and focus on its texture, weight, and temperature.
Use the object as a reminder to stay grounded in the present.
Sit comfortably and close your eyes.
Take slow, deep breaths.
As you inhale, imagine breathing in calmness and peace.
As you exhale, imagine releasing tension and anxiety.
Visualize yourself rooted to the ground like a tree, with your worries flowing away like leaves in the wind.
Close your eyes and focus on your breath for a few moments.
Gradually bring your attention to different parts of your body, starting from your toes and moving upward.
Notice any sensations, tension, or discomfort without trying to change them.
As you breathe, imagine releasing tension with each exhale, allowing your body to feel more relaxed and grounded.
Take a slow walk, focusing on each step you take.
Feel the ground beneath your feet and the movement of your body.
Notice the sights, sounds, and sensations around you as you move.
Physical movement can help release pent-up energy and promote a sense of grounding.
